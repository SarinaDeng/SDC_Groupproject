Introduction


To further analyse the ralationship between different characteristics of commuters and how they choose commuting modes, we employed a series of methods including stepwise regression and DBSCAN clustering.


We divided the characteristics of people into five groups according to their age, level of qualification, distance to work, working hours and gender. From original data, variables are grouped into several sections. For example, ages are grouped into 16-24, 25-34, 35-49, 50-64 and over 65. We took 34 as a turning point, and calculated the percentage of people who are under 34 to be considered as young people. Followed by this rule, we also calculated the percentage of people who have higher qualification than level 4, work less than 10km to workplace, work as full time and are men. In addition, the ratios of people who choose one specific commute method to total population in that area were calculated.

Stepwise regression is one way to help us find the most relevant predictors while excluding irrelevant ones. Python was used to achieve stepwise regression, and the basic codes were given by the course. Entering and removing p-value thresholds were set as 0.10 and 0.15.

  • Selected predictors


    Public transports--Distance (negative), age


    Personal transports--Gender, qualification and full time


    Others (including walking and riding bikes)--Distance, age and full-time (negative)


  • For ages, young people tend to use more public and other transports.


  • For qualifications, people with higher qualifications tend to use more personal transports.


  • For working distance, people live closer to the workplace tend to use more other transport while people live further tend to use more public transports.


  • For working time, people work as full-time tend to use more personal transports and less other transports.


  • For genders, there is no obvious difference in choosing commute methods by men and women. However, men tend to use more personal transports than women.

DBSCAN Cluster

We could gain some insight into the structure of the data by clustering. Meanwhile, the clustering results combined with geographic information data could help us learn more about regional distribution and the regularity of the clusters.

Percentages of commuters who took public, personal and other transports were clustered with other variables in turn.

Conclusion

  • Commuting modes in London are bounded by the zone 2. There are obvious differences outside and within zone 2.
  • Inside zone 2, public and other transport are the main methods. Outside zone 2, personal transport is the main commuting method.
  • This may be because the central area has a better public transportation network and commuters who live there are closer to their working destinations. Commuters who live outside zone 2 may face longer distance, so personal transports are better.
  • The proportion of male commuters is higher in the central area, which seems to influence the way people choose to commute.
  • The results of clustering provide good references for regression analysis and data visualisation.
